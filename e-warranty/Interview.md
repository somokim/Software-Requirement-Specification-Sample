### Interview: Customer / Product Owner (S01)

**Q1: Walk me through the last time you registered a product warranty — what did you actually have to do, step by step, from the moment you opened the box?**

So I bought a new washing machine about four months ago. Inside the box there was a folded warranty card — small print, tiny fields. I had to fill in my name, address, the model number, serial number, and the retailer name and purchase date. The serial number was on a sticker on the back of the machine, which had already been pushed against the wall during installation. I had to pull the machine out to read it. Then I was supposed to post the card back. I actually never did. I took a photo of it thinking I'd do it later and then just... forgot. I'm not even sure my warranty is registered. I have no confirmation either way.

**Q2: When you needed to check whether a product was still under warranty, how did you find that information, and how long did it take?**

I had a cordless vacuum that stopped working. I had no idea if it was still under warranty. I dug through a drawer where I keep manuals and receipts — took about 20 minutes. Found the receipt but not the warranty card. Called the manufacturer's customer service line, waited on hold for about 12 minutes, then had to read out the serial number, give my purchase date, and they told me verbally it was still covered — but they couldn't send me anything in writing confirming that unless I submitted a claim. So I had no real proof. Just a phone call.

**Q3: Have you ever tried to make a warranty claim? If so, what documents or proof did you need to gather, and where did that process break down or frustrate you?**

Yes — for a laptop charger that failed after eight months. They asked for the original receipt, proof of purchase from the retailer's system, the serial number of the laptop, and photos of the fault. I had to email a scanned receipt, which I had to dig out of my filing cabinet and scan at a library because I don't own a scanner. The photo they asked for was hard to provide because the fault was intermittent — it only failed sometimes, not visibly. They came back asking for more photos. I sent the same ones again with a written explanation. It took 19 days to get a response and in the end they approved it, but the process was completely disproportionate to the value of the item.

**Q4: What details about a product do you typically struggle to locate when asked to provide them during registration?**

The serial number, without question. It's almost always in a ridiculous location — underneath the device, behind a panel, on a sticker that's partially worn off. For electronics it's sometimes only in the packaging, which most people throw away. Model numbers I can usually find in a settings menu or on the front of the product, but serial numbers feel deliberately hidden. I've also been asked for a "batch code" before and had absolutely no idea what that was or where to find it.

**Q5: If you submitted a warranty claim and heard nothing back for several days, what would you do — who would you contact and through what channel?**

After about five days with no acknowledgement, I'd go back to whatever channel I used originally — usually email — and send a follow-up. If I got nothing after another two days, I'd call. I don't like calling because of the hold times, but email clearly isn't getting results at that point. If I still got nowhere, I'd honestly consider disputing it through my credit card provider or posting about it publicly. The lack of a tracking reference or acknowledgement number is what makes it so frustrating — you have nothing to quote, nothing to reference, and no way to know if it was received at all.

**Q6: Have you ever lost a paper warranty card, missed a registration deadline, or been told your warranty was invalid for a reason you didn't expect?**

Yes. I bought a power tool and the warranty card had a 30-day registration window — I didn't even know that was a condition until I tried to claim 14 months later and was told registration had expired. That was nowhere obvious on the packaging. I was told the warranty was void because I hadn't registered in time. I was furious. I had a clear proof of purchase, the product was clearly faulty, but because of an administrative step I'd missed through no clear fault of my own, I got nothing. That kind of thing makes people distrust warranties entirely.

**Q7: When it comes to receiving updates about your warranty — expiry reminders, claim progress notifications — what channel do you actually want to hear from?**

Email is fine for reminders — maybe one email about 60 days before expiry and one at 30 days. That's enough. I don't want texts unless something urgent has changed on a claim I've submitted. App notifications I'd turn off immediately. And please, one email per event. I don't want a weekly "warranty newsletter." If I've submitted a claim, I want a status update email every time something actually changes — not silence for two weeks followed by a final decision with no explanation of what happened in between.

**Q8: What would make you feel genuinely confident that your warranty claim was being handled seriously?**

An automatic acknowledgement with a reference number the moment I submit — that alone would already make me feel better than most current processes. Then a clear timeline: "You'll hear from us within 5 business days." Then an actual update at that point, even if it's just "still under review." And a name — even a team name — not just "The Warranty Team." I want to feel like a person looked at it, not like it went into a void. If I could log in and see the status myself without having to call anyone, that would be ideal.

**Q9: If you could change one thing about how warranty registration and claims work today, what would it be?**

Automatic registration at point of purchase. If I bought the product from a retailer that already has my details, the warranty should register itself. I shouldn't have to do anything. The serial number is scanned at the till — that data exists. The idea that I have to separately fill in a card with information the retailer already captured is completely backwards. If that's not possible, then at minimum: scan a QR code in the box, confirm my email, done. Thirty seconds maximum. No forms, no serial number hunting, no post.

### Interview: Warranty Claims Processor (S02)

**Q1: Take me through exactly what happens from the moment a warranty claim lands on your desk — what is the first thing you check and what are each of the steps you follow?**

When a claim comes in — currently by email or a paper form handed in at the service counter — the first thing I do is check whether all the mandatory fields are complete. Name, contact details, product model, serial number, purchase date, and a description of the fault. If anything is missing, I send a standard request-for-information email and flag the claim as "pending customer response" in our spreadsheet. If it's complete, I open our product register — which is a separate Excel file maintained by the product team — and verify the model number exists and what the warranty period is for that product. Then I cross-reference the purchase date against today's date to confirm the warranty hasn't expired. After that I check the serial number against any previous claims to see if that unit has been claimed on before. That check I do by manually searching the claims history spreadsheet. Then I assess the described fault — is it covered under the standard warranty terms, or does it fall under exclusions like accidental damage or user misuse? If everything checks out, I approve and log it. If there's a red flag, I escalate or reject.

**Q2: What information do you need to verify eligibility, and where do you currently go to find each piece?**

Purchase date and retailer — customer-provided, I take it at face value unless something looks suspicious. Warranty period for that product — I look it up in the Excel product register. Serial number history — I search the claims spreadsheet manually. Whether the fault is covered — I refer to a printed warranty terms document we have in a folder, which was last updated about 18 months ago and I'm honestly not certain it reflects current terms for our newer product lines. Proof of purchase — customer emails a photo or PDF, I save it to a shared folder with a naming convention that I made up myself because there's no defined standard. I've been doing this for three years and I've built most of these processes myself because there was no formal training.

**Q3: When a customer's details don't match what's on record — serial number mismatch, disputed purchase date — what do you do?**

First I try to resolve it myself. If the serial number is close — like one digit off — I sometimes assume it's a transcription error and contact the customer to confirm. If the purchase date looks suspicious — for example, the product was only released six months ago and they're claiming they bought it 14 months ago — I'll ask for additional proof of purchase. If the customer pushes back or it gets complicated, I escalate to my supervisor. That escalation currently means walking over to her desk or sending an email, and it can sit unresolved for days if she's busy. There's no formal escalation workflow. I've had claims sit in a grey zone for three weeks because nobody officially owned the decision.

**Q4: How do you currently manage your claims queue?**

I work through them in order of receipt — oldest first. I keep a running spreadsheet that I update manually. Each row is a claim, and I colour-code them: green for approved, red for rejected, yellow for pending, orange for escalated. I update it myself in real time as I process. The problem is that when a colleague is off sick, I inherit their queue but I have to work from their version of the spreadsheet, which doesn't always follow the same format as mine. Last month two claims were processed twice because of a merge conflict between two versions of the file. The customer was confused, and so were we.

**Q5: What are the most common reasons you reject a claim?**

In order: warranty period expired, fault described is excluded under the terms (usually wear and tear or accidental damage), serial number doesn't match any registered product, and proof of purchase either not provided or clearly invalid. The grey area cases — and they come up maybe 10 to 15 times a month — are things like: a customer bought a product as a gift and has no receipt in their name, or the product was purchased through a third-party marketplace and we're not certain our warranty applies. Those I find genuinely difficult because the terms don't explicitly cover every scenario, and I don't always feel confident making the call myself.

**Q6: Describe a scenario where a claim went significantly wrong.**

About a year ago I rejected a claim for an out-of-warranty product. The customer had actually registered the warranty, and the registration extended their cover by 12 months — which I wasn't aware of at the time because registration data and claims data are in completely separate systems and I don't have easy access to the registration records. The customer escalated to our customer service team, who escalated to my manager, who eventually found the registration record. I'd rejected a valid claim. We had to reopen it, process it, and apologise to the customer. The root cause was that I simply had no visibility of registration data when processing the claim. That gap hasn't been fixed.

**Q7: Are there manual steps you do today that you believe serve no purpose beyond filling a system gap?**

Yes. I manually copy the approved claim details from our spreadsheet into a separate Word template to generate a confirmation letter, which I then save as a PDF and email to the customer. That entire step could be automated. I also manually update a tally sheet at the end of each day with how many claims I processed, approved, rejected, and escalated — my manager uses this for her weekly report. It takes me about 20 minutes a day and it's entirely redundant if the data is already in a system. And every Friday I print a claims summary and put it in a physical folder in the filing cabinet. I have no idea if anyone ever reads it.

**Q8: What does a good day look like in terms of throughput?**

On a clean day, with no missing information and no escalations, I can comfortably process about 25 to 30 claims. When I'm chasing information from customers or waiting on escalation decisions, that number drops to 10 to 15. The thing that kills throughput most is incomplete submissions — probably 35 to 40% of claims that come in are missing something. If the system enforced mandatory fields at submission, that number would drop dramatically and I'd be significantly more productive.

**Q9: Is there information you wish you had at the point of reviewing a claim that you currently have to go and ask someone else for?**

Yes — the customer's full warranty registration record, including when they registered, which retailer they purchased from, and whether they've had any previous claims on that serial number. Right now I have no access to registration data. I also wish I had a quick-reference summary of what's covered under the warranty for each specific product — not the full 12-page terms document, but a plain-language summary of the key inclusions and exclusions. And frankly, I'd love to know the customer's contact history — whether they've already called customer service about this issue — so I'm not processing a claim cold without knowing there's already been a conversation elsewhere.

### Interview: Warranty Operations Manager (S03)

**Q1: How do you currently monitor your team's claims processing workload?**

Honestly, it's quite manual and reactive. Each processor maintains their own spreadsheet and at the end of each day they email me a summary — number received, processed, pending, escalated. I consolidate those into a master sheet every morning, which takes me about 30 to 40 minutes. I have no live view at all. If a processor gets swamped at 2pm on a Tuesday, I won't know until I get their end-of-day report. By then, claims may already be breaching our 5-day turnaround commitment. I've asked IT twice for a shared dashboard but it hasn't been prioritised. So right now I manage by walking the floor and asking people how they're doing — which is not a scalable monitoring strategy.

**Q2: What SLAs govern warranty claim processing, and how do you know when one is about to be breached?**

Our internal commitment is 5 business days from receipt to decision. We also have a customer communication standard — customers should receive an acknowledgement within 24 hours. In practice the acknowledgement step is manual, so it often doesn't happen until the processor actually starts working the claim, which can be day two or three. For the 5-day SLA, I currently have no automated alerting. I only know a claim is approaching breach if a processor flags it to me or if a customer calls to complain. I've had situations where I discovered a claim breached because the customer rang customer service 9 days after submitting and had heard nothing. That's not a good way to find out.

**Q3: When a high volume of claims comes in, how do you manage capacity?**

We have three processors. If volume spikes — and it does after product recalls or major promotional periods — I manually redistribute work. I look at each person's queue count and move the oldest unassigned items to whoever has capacity. That process is done over email and IM, which is chaotic. We don't have a formal overflow protocol. I've cross-trained two people from the customer service team to assist during peaks, but they're not familiar with all the product lines and their error rate is higher, so I have to review their approvals manually, which actually adds to my own workload. During our last peak period in November we had a 3-day backlog and I was personally reviewing 20 to 30 claims a day on top of my management responsibilities.

**Q4: Which decisions do you make daily that you currently don't have the data to make confidently?**

Staffing allocation — I can't make a data-driven decision about whether I need three processors or four because I have no reliable throughput baseline. I also make decisions about whether to approve borderline claims — cases my processors escalate where the fault is arguable — without any consistent reference framework. I rely on experience and gut feel, which means two similar claims might get different outcomes depending on which day they were escalated and what my mood was. That inconsistency worries me from a compliance and fairness standpoint. I also can't confidently tell leadership how long the average claim takes to resolve because I don't actually track time-in-process at a granular level — I only know date received and date closed.

**Q5: How are escalated or disputed claims handled?**

There's no formal escalation pathway documented. In practice, if a processor is unsure, they come to me verbally or via email. I make a call and tell them to proceed. That decision is not recorded anywhere — not in the claim record, not in any log. If someone later asks why that claim was approved or rejected, I'd have to reconstruct the reasoning from memory or email threads. For external disputes — customer formally complains or threatens legal action — I loop in our Legal team, but that's an ad hoc conversation, not a structured handoff. There are no defined timeframes for escalation resolution. I've had escalated claims sit open for two weeks because I was waiting on a legal opinion that wasn't formally requested.

**Q6: When something goes wrong operationally, how do you find out?**

Usually from a customer complaint landing in customer service and being forwarded to me. Occasionally from a processor flagging something they spotted themselves. Rarely do I proactively catch errors before they affect the customer. I had a situation last quarter where a processor inadvertently approved a batch of claims that should have been pended because a product recall had been issued — the recall notice was circulated by product management via email, but the processor had been on annual leave and missed it. Twelve claims were approved that should have been held. We had to issue corrections and re-contact customers. There was no mechanism to broadcast a processing hold to active queues. That needs to be a feature, frankly.

**Q7: What does your current reporting cycle look like?**

I produce a weekly operational report — manually, in Excel — covering claims received, processed, approved, rejected, and pending by processor. I send it to the Head of Operations every Monday morning. It takes me between 90 minutes and two hours to compile because I'm pulling data from three processor spreadsheets, checking for duplicates, and formatting it. Monthly I produce a more detailed report with trend data, average processing times, and escalation rates. That takes half a day. I'm spending time I should be using to manage my team producing spreadsheet reports that a system should be generating automatically.

**Q8: If you had real-time visibility of your team's operations, what are the three numbers you'd want to see first?**

Number one: claims currently in queue, by processor and by age — specifically how many are older than three business days, because that's my early warning for an SLA breach. Number two: claims awaiting customer response — because those inflate my queue numbers but aren't actionable by my team, and I want to see them separately. Number three: escalations currently open and how long each has been open — because that's where things go silent and fall through the cracks, and right now I have no visibility into that at all unless someone tells me.

**Q9: What does the current process cost your team in administrative overhead?**

I'd estimate my three processors collectively spend about 2 hours per day on non-processing tasks — manual data entry, spreadsheet updates, generating confirmation letters, filing. That's 6 hours of processing capacity lost per day to administrative overhead. For me personally, roughly 4 hours per week goes on report compilation alone. If I add up the time spent chasing missing claim information from customers, which every processor does individually by writing separate emails, we're probably losing another 2 to 3 hours per day across the team. A system that automated acknowledgements, enforced mandatory fields, and generated reports would effectively give me back the equivalent of one full-time processor.

### Interview: Customer Service Representative (S04)

**Q1: When a customer calls about their warranty, what information do you need and how many places do you look?**

Typically I need their name, the product model, the serial number, and ideally their purchase date. To look up their record I currently have to check three different places: our CRM system for customer contact history, a shared drive folder where warranty registrations are stored as scanned PDF forms, and a separate claims spreadsheet that the warranty team maintains. The CRM doesn't link to the warranty records at all — they're completely disconnected. On a good day it takes me about 3 to 4 minutes just to pull together a complete picture before I can even start answering the customer's question. During that time the customer is waiting, which is not a great experience and inflates my handling time.

**Q2: What happens when a customer wants to submit a claim through you?**

I take their details manually on a notepad, then I fill in the claim form template — a Word document we have saved on the shared drive — on their behalf. I save it as a PDF, email it to the warranty team inbox, and tell the customer to expect a response within 5 business days. I have no way of tracking whether that claim was actually received and actioned by the warranty team. I have no claim reference number to give the customer. If they call back two days later asking for an update, I have to email the warranty team inbox and wait for someone to reply — sometimes that takes a day. So the customer calls me, I have nothing, I call back. It's a lot of manual handoff with no audit trail.

**Q3: What are the most common questions you cannot answer immediately?**

Whether their warranty is still active — I can see a registration record if one exists, but I can't always tell from the scanned form what the exact expiry date is. Whether their specific fault is covered — I have a general sense of what's excluded but I don't have a quick-reference guide, so I sometimes have to put customers on hold to ask a colleague or check the warranty terms document. And claim status — if a customer is chasing a claim they submitted by email or post, I have no live view of the claims queue. I have to relay messages between the customer and the warranty team, which slows everything down and makes me look unhelpful.

**Q4: How do you handle situations where a customer insists they're covered but your records say otherwise?**

First I try to verify what I can — I ask for their proof of purchase and check our registration records again carefully. If the records clearly show they're outside the warranty period, I explain that politely and offer to escalate to the warranty manager if they want to dispute it. If the records are ambiguous — for example, the registration date is unclear on a scanned form, or the handwriting is hard to read — I log it as a query and ask a warranty team member to review. That review can take a day or two. I document my conversation notes in the CRM but there's no formal flag on the warranty record itself, so the warranty team doesn't always have that context when they look at it. Twice I've had the warranty team close a query as rejected without seeing my notes, and then I've had to reopen it.

**Q5: What percentage of your warranty-related contacts are follow-ups on something already submitted?**

Honestly I'd estimate about 45 to 50%. Half of my warranty calls are people who submitted something and heard nothing. The most common thing I hear is "I sent an email two weeks ago and no one responded." Sometimes when I check the warranty inbox they did respond but the email went to the customer's spam folder. Sometimes the email was genuinely never replied to. Either way the customer is frustrated before the call even starts, and I'm managing that frustration without having the information to actually resolve it. If customers had a way to check their own claim status, I genuinely believe my warranty-related call volume would drop by at least a third.

**Q6: Describe the worst type of warranty interaction you regularly face.**

The worst is when a customer has been told different things by different people. They called two weeks ago, someone told them their claim was approved. Then they called last week and someone else — possibly me — told them it was still under review. Now they're calling again, angry and confused. When I look at the records, I can see my CRM note from last week but I can't see what was said in the first call because it was handled by a different agent who logged it differently — or didn't log it at all. There's no single source of truth. The customer's experience is completely inconsistent and I have no way to reconcile the conflicting information they've been given. That type of call can take 20 minutes and there's no clean resolution.

**Q7: Are there any commitments you make during calls that you then have to manually track?**

Yes. If I tell a customer "I'll have someone call you back within 48 hours," I write that in my personal notebook and try to check it the next day. There's nothing in the CRM that flags this as a follow-up task linked to the warranty record. I also sometimes tell customers I'll email them a confirmation of what we discussed — I write that email manually from scratch, there's no template. I've missed follow-ups before. Not often, but it's happened, and when it does the customer calls back even more frustrated. A proper system would let me set a follow-up reminder linked to the customer's record and auto-generate a confirmation email.

**Q8: What information would you want visible the moment you open a customer's profile?**

Their current active warranties — product name, purchase date, and expiry date — at the top of the screen immediately. Any open claims with a current status. Any previous claims history. Any recent contact — when they last called, what it was about, what was resolved. And any flags — for example, if this customer has an ongoing dispute or has been escalated previously. All of that in one screen, without me having to navigate to three different systems. Right now I'm stitching that picture together from multiple sources mid-call while trying to have a conversation. It's not sustainable.

### Interview: Product Manager (S05)

**Q1: How do you currently receive information about warranty claims related to your products?**

Infrequently and inconsistently. Once a month I get a summary from the Operations Manager — a spreadsheet with total claims by product category. That's the extent of my formal reporting. If I want anything more granular — claims by specific model, or fault type, or within a particular date range — I have to email the warranty team and ask them to pull it manually. That request usually takes 3 to 5 days to come back, and the format varies depending on who pulls it. I don't have direct access to any warranty data. I'm entirely dependent on Operations to interpret and surface it for me, which means I'm always working with a delay and always limited by what they think to include.

**Q2: What data points would allow you to identify whether a fault is one-off or systemic?**

I'd want to see claim volume by fault type as a percentage of units sold — not raw numbers, because a product with 50,000 units in the field will naturally have more claims than one with 5,000. I'd want to segment by manufacturing date or batch code, because systemic issues often track back to a specific production run. I'd also want to see the average time-to-failure — how many months after purchase the fault is occurring — because early failure patterns are a different signal from late-lifecycle failures. Right now I have none of that. I get total claim counts. That's it. I'm essentially flying blind when it comes to product quality signals from the field.

**Q3: Have you ever discovered a product quality problem through warranty data after it had already been affecting customers for a significant period?**

Yes, and it's still a sore point. About 18 months ago we had a motor component failing in one of our mid-range product lines. Claims were coming in steadily for about four months before I was made aware. By the time Operations flagged it to me, we had over 200 claims on that model. The delay happened because nobody was looking at the data with product quality in mind — the warranty team was processing claims individually and didn't see the pattern, and I wasn't receiving any reporting. When I finally got the data and traced it back to a manufacturing batch, we'd already shipped further units with the same component. The supplier remediation cost and the reputational damage from that episode would likely have been significantly reduced with earlier detection.

**Q4: When you need to compare warranty claim rates across product variants, what does that process look like?**

I request the data from the warranty team — as I mentioned, a multi-day wait. I also have to request sales volumes from the commercial team to calculate a claims rate. Those two datasets come back in different formats and I spend time reconciling them in Excel. Often the product naming conventions don't match between systems — the warranty team might refer to a product by a model code while the sales data uses a product description. I've spent entire afternoons just cleaning data before I can start the actual analysis. On a good run, with prompt responses, I can have a basic comparison ready in about a week. That's far too slow for the business decisions I need to make.

**Q5: Are there specific fault categories or codes you'd want standardised?**

Absolutely — this is one of my biggest frustrations. Currently, fault descriptions are free text entered by the customer or the claims processor. "Motor not working," "motor failure," "stopped spinning," and "won't turn on" might all describe the same underlying fault, but they'd never group together in any analysis. I've been asking for a standardised fault taxonomy for two years. At minimum I'd want a structured field for fault category — electrical, mechanical, structural, cosmetic — with a secondary code for specific fault type. That alone would transform our ability to spot patterns. Without it, any reporting on fault trends is essentially manual text analysis, which doesn't scale.

**Q6: What do you do when warranty data suggests a product is underperforming?**

I document the data, write a brief summary of my analysis, and request a meeting with the Head of Product and the Supply Chain team. In that meeting I present the evidence and propose either a supplier review, a design change request, or in serious cases a product hold. The evidence threshold for a supplier review is roughly a 3% claim rate within the first 12 months; for a product hold it would need to be higher and I'd need Legal involved. The challenge is that by the time I've assembled the evidence from the current fragmented data sources, weeks have passed. I'm essentially advocating for action based on old data, and the pace of decision-making suffers because of it.

**Q7: Is there any warranty insight you believe the organisation is currently failing to capture?**

Geographic distribution of claims. We sell nationally but I have no idea if certain regions generate disproportionate claims — which could indicate a logistics issue, a retailer misrepresenting the product, or even a climate or usage pattern factor. We're also not capturing anything about the customer's usage context — commercial use versus domestic use — which is relevant because our warranties explicitly exclude commercial use of domestically rated products, but we have no way to detect or track that. And we're completely missing the repeat-customer angle — if a customer has claimed twice on different products, that's either very bad luck or a pattern we should be aware of, and right now that's invisible to us.

**Q8: How would you want warranty reporting surfaced to you?**

A dashboard I can access myself, with the ability to filter by product line and date range without having to request data from anyone. I'd also want an automated alert when a claim rate for any model crosses a threshold — say, 2% within the first 6 months post-launch — so I can investigate early rather than waiting for a monthly report. Scheduled reports are fine for routine monitoring, but for exception-based events I want to be notified in near real-time. Right now I find out about problems months after they've started. That lag has commercial and reputational consequences.

### Interview: Business Sponsor / Executive Owner (S06)

**Q1: What is the primary business problem this system must solve, and how will you measure success?**

We are haemorrhaging operational cost and customer goodwill through a process that hasn't been modernised in a decade. Customers are registering warranties on paper cards that get lost, claims take too long to process, and we have no visibility of what's happening at any level — not the volume, not the turnaround, not the outcome patterns. The primary problem is operational inefficiency compounded by poor customer experience. Success for me is measurable on three dimensions 12 months post-go-live: a 40% reduction in average claims processing time compared to baseline, a warranty registration rate above 60% of units sold — we're currently around 22% — and a measurable improvement in post-purchase customer satisfaction scores specifically related to warranty interactions.

**Q2: Are there specific cost reduction targets tied to this project?**

Yes. We've quantified the current manual processing cost at approximately £8.50 per claim when you factor in staff time, postage, and paper administration. Our target is to reduce that to under £3.00 per claim through automation. We process approximately 14,000 claims annually, so the saving at target efficiency is in the range of £77,000 per year — recurring. Additionally, we're spending an estimated £40,000 per year on storage and retrieval of physical warranty documentation. Eliminating paper entirely is a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have. The business case was approved on the basis of a 24-month payback period. I'll be reviewing that at the 12-month mark.

**Q3: What is the strategic context behind this project?**

This is part of a broader digital customer experience transformation we committed to in our three-year strategy. Warranty is one of three workstreams; the others are post-sale support scheduling and customer loyalty. There is also a compliance dimension — our legal team has flagged that our current paper-based processes create data retention risks under GDPR, and that exposure needs to be addressed. So there's a regulatory driver as well as a commercial one. From a competitive standpoint, two of our main competitors have already moved to digital warranty management and we're visibly behind. I've seen it come up in customer feedback surveys.

**Q4: Are there any non-negotiable delivery milestones?**

The system must be live — at minimum for warranty registration and claim submission — before our peak sales period, which begins in the first week of October. That is a hard date. Missing it means another full year operating on the current model during peak volume, which is not acceptable. I've also committed to the board that we will present initial operational performance data from the new system at the February board meeting. So the go-live window is effectively early September at the latest to allow for stabilisation before October. That is firm.

**Q5: Who has authority to make final decisions if there are conflicting requirements between departments?**

I do, ultimately, as the project sponsor. But I want that escalation to be infrequent. I'm expecting the Operations Manager and the IT Project Manager to resolve 90% of conflicts between themselves. If something reaches me, it should be a genuine strategic trade-off — not a disagreement about screen layout. I'll be available for a monthly governance review and I expect to be briefed on any scope change requests before they're approved or declined. My expectation is that the BA team manages stakeholder alignment proactively so that I'm not being asked to adjudicate operational disagreements.

**Q6: What level of executive visibility do you want post go-live?**

For the serial number validation call, if the product catalogue is unavailable, I need a business decision: do we fail the registration hard, or do we allow provisional registration with a manual review flag? **Q9: What does a well-written requirement look like to you?**

### Interview: QA / Test Engineer (S09)

**Q1: For each major feature, what does a fully tested feature look like to you?**

For warranty registration: I'd need to be able to test successful registration with all valid data, every mandatory field individually absent, duplicate registration of the same serial number, registration of a serial number that doesn't exist in the product catalogue, registration close to and on the exact expiry date of an eligibility window, and the confirmation communication being triggered correctly. For claim submission: valid claim, invalid claim for each rejection reason, claim against an expired warranty, claim resubmission after rejection. For the approval workflow: every status transition — submitted to under review, to approved, to rejected, to escalated and back — and the actions permitted at each state by each role. For notifications: date-triggered events at exact boundaries — 60 days before expiry, 30 days, day of expiry. A "fully tested" feature means I have a test case for every path through the logic, including errors. If I don't have acceptance criteria, I can't define those paths.

**Q2: What are the most common sources of defects on projects like this?**

Vague acceptance criteria is number one — "the system shall display an error message" is not testable. Error message for what? What does it say? Where is it displayed? The second is missing edge cases in requirements — typically around date boundaries, null values, and workflow state transitions. The third — and this one is particularly painful — is late requirement changes during build. When a developer is halfway through implementing a workflow and the business stakeholder changes a rule, the developer adjusts their code, forgets to tell QA, and I write tests against the old behaviour. I find the defect myself in testing, which is the best case. The worst case is the defect reaches UAT or production. Here, I'm most concerned about the notification logic and the claims workflow state machine — both are complex enough that partial changes mid-build would be very difficult to test comprehensively.

**Q3: How do you handle testing for notification logic?**

Notification testing is always fiddly. I need the test environment to support date manipulation — I need to be able to set a system date to 61 days before a warranty expiry and verify the email is triggered, then set it to 30 days and verify again. If the test environment runs on real system time, I can't test these scenarios practically without waiting. I also need access to an email testing tool — something like Mailhog or Mailtrap — so I can inspect the content and formatting of outbound emails without sending to real customers. From requirements, I need: the exact trigger conditions for each notification type, the exact timing, the content requirements for each email including any dynamic fields, and what happens if a notification fails to send — is it retried, logged, or silently dropped? All of that needs to be in the requirements document, not discovered during build.

**Q4: What workflow boundary conditions do requirements often fail to specify?**

Consistently: what happens when a claim is submitted for an already-rejected claim — is it treated as a new claim or a resubmission? What happens when the same serial number is submitted for a second claim within the warranty period — is there a limit on claims per unit? What happens when a warranty expires while a claim is actively being processed — does the claim continue under the terms at submission date or does expiry invalidate it mid-process? What happens when a customer account is deleted while a claim is open? What happens when a processor approves a claim and then realises they made an error — is there a reversal mechanism, and within what timeframe? Every one of those will generate a defect in UAT if not addressed in requirements. I can write them down now as a test preparation list, but they need answers from the business before testing begins.

**Q5: For the claim approval workflow, what states and transitions would you need documented?**

At minimum: Submitted, Acknowledged, Under Review, Information Requested, Information Received, Escalated, Approved, Rejected, Closed. For each state I need: who can enter this state, what triggers the transition, what actions are available from this state, what communications are triggered on entry, and what the system does if the state is not progressed within a defined timeframe. I also need the complete transition matrix — which states can move to which other states. For example, can an Approved claim be moved back to Under Review? Can a Rejected claim be reopened? Can an Escalated claim be directly Approved without going back to Under Review? These aren't obvious and different stakeholders will have different expectations. Without a confirmed state machine, I cannot write a complete workflow test suite.

**Q6: When you discover a requirement is ambiguous during testing, how is it resolved?**

In this organisation it goes through the BA, who raises it with the relevant business stakeholder, who gives a verbal answer, which the BA documents and sends round. Best case that takes two days. Worst case — if the stakeholder is unavailable or the ambiguity involves multiple stakeholders with different views — it can take a week or more. During that time the developer is typically blocked or makes an assumption, and the test is suspended. If the resolution changes what was built, the developer fixes it, it goes back into test, and the test cycle extends. On our last project this pattern contributed to a 3-week overrun on the testing phase. The mitigation is to resolve ambiguities before build, not during test. Which is exactly why I'd rather review requirements documents before sprint one than raise issues when a feature is already coded.

**Q7: What level of performance testing is expected?**

It hasn't been formally discussed yet, which is a risk. Based on the volumes I've heard — 270 claims per week normally, up to 1,000 during peaks, potentially thousands of concurrent portal visitors during a promotional event — I'd want to run a load test simulating at least 500 concurrent users on the registration portal and 100 concurrent internal users on the claims dashboard. I'd also want a soak test — sustained load over several hours — to identify memory leaks or degradation over time. I have JMeter available for load testing and a shared performance environment that we use for other applications. What I don't have is a confirmed performance baseline — the business needs to tell me what response time is acceptable. "The system should be fast" is not a testable requirement.

**Q8: What would requirements documentation need to contain for you to write a complete regression suite before build?**

Every functional requirement must have at least one positive acceptance criterion and one negative acceptance criterion. Every workflow must have a state diagram or transition table. Every notification must have its trigger condition, timing, and content fully specified. Every field on every form must have its data type, length, format, and validation rule documented. Every role must have its permissions explicitly listed — not inferred from descriptions. And there must be a clear definition of what constitutes successful completion for each business process. If I have all of that, I can write the regression suite in parallel with development and be ready to test the moment the first feature is ready. ### Interview: Data Analyst / BI Analyst (S10)

**Q1: What warranty-related questions can the business currently not answer because the data doesn't exist or isn't in a usable form?**

Several critical ones. We cannot tell you the actual warranty registration rate as a percentage of units sold — we have registration counts but no reliable link to sales volumes. We cannot tell you the average time from claim submission to resolution — we have submission dates and close dates on some records but the data is incomplete and inconsistently recorded. We cannot tell you the most common fault type because fault descriptions are free text and unstandardised. We cannot identify products with statistically elevated claim rates against their installed base because we don't have per-product sales data linked to warranty data. And we cannot track a customer's full warranty lifecycle — registration through to claim resolution — because those events are stored in separate, unconnected systems. Almost every strategically useful question about warranty performance is currently unanswerable from our data.

**Q2: Walk me through how you currently produce a warranty performance report.**

It starts on Thursday afternoon for a Monday morning delivery. I email the warranty team asking for a claims export from their spreadsheet. I email the product team asking for a sales summary by SKU. I usually chase at least one of those on Friday. By Monday morning I have two or three Excel files in slightly different formats. I spend the first hour cleaning them — removing blank rows, standardising model name formats, resolving naming mismatches. Then I build a pivot table manually. I calculate claim rates, format it into a template I built about two years ago, and export to PDF. The whole process takes roughly four to five hours per monthly report. The data is already 1 to 2 weeks old by the time I receive it. The report I deliver on Monday reflects the state of claims as of the previous Wednesday at best.

**Q3: What dimensions do stakeholders most commonly want to slice warranty data by?**

Product category and specific model are the most common — Product Managers want that immediately. Time period — monthly trends, year-on-year comparisons — is consistently requested. Claim type and fault category would be hugely valuable if those fields existed in a structured form, which they currently don't. Retailer or purchase channel is requested occasionally — particularly when a specific retailer's customers seem to have elevated claim rates, which can indicate a mis-selling or product handling issue at the retailer level. And resolution outcome — approved versus rejected, and reason for rejection — is important for compliance reporting. None of these are currently available as structured, queryable data. Most of them have to be manually constructed from free text or reconstructed from memory.

**Q4: What is the most common ad hoc data request and how long does it take to fulfil?**

"How many claims have we had on [specific product] in the last [X] months?" That request comes in at least twice a week from product managers and senior stakeholders. Currently it takes me between 2 and 4 hours because I have to pull the raw data, clean it, filter it, and format the output. With a properly structured database and a self-service reporting interface, a product manager should be able to answer that question themselves in under 30 seconds. The fact that it routes through me is an inefficiency I'd like to eliminate. My value should be in interpreting data and identifying insights, not in pulling basic filtered counts for people who could pull them themselves with the right tools.

**Q5: Are there data quality issues in the current process that this system must address?**

Multiple. Duplicate registrations — the same serial number registered more than once, either by the same customer or by different customers, and there's no deduplication logic currently. Free text fault descriptions that make any trend analysis manual and unreliable. Missing purchase dates — customers often leave that field blank and it goes in as null, which makes warranty period validation impossible. Inconsistent model naming — "WM-3000," "WM3000," and "Washing Machine Model 3000" all refer to the same product but would never group together in a query. And retailer data — currently free text, so we have hundreds of variations of the same retailer name. The new system must enforce structured, validated inputs at the point of data entry. Garbage in, garbage out — and right now the input is almost entirely garbage.

**Q6: When a report contradicts what a stakeholder expects to see, what typically happens?**

They question the data, not their expectations. Almost universally. I've had a Product Manager tell me my claim rate figures were "wrong" because they were higher than she expected — the figures were correct, the expectation was the problem. But because data credibility is low in this organisation — people know our data is messy — there's always reasonable doubt, and that doubt gets directed at the analyst rather than the process. It's demoralising and it slows down decision-making. Decisions get deferred pending "data verification" that often just means asking me to rerun the same analysis in a slightly different format until the answer looks like what someone expected. A system with a reliable, auditable data trail would go a long way toward rebuilding trust in the numbers.

**Q7: What export or integration capabilities do you need?**

Ideally the system would write to a central data warehouse — we use Snowflake — so that warranty data can be combined with sales, customer, and product data in a single analytical environment. If a direct warehouse integration isn't feasible in phase one, I'd accept a scheduled CSV or JSON export to a defined SFTP location that I can pick up with our ETL pipeline. In-system reporting alone is not sufficient for my needs — the business questions I need to answer require warranty data combined with data from other sources, which means it needs to be extractable in a format that's compatible with our existing data stack. **Q8: Are there regulatory or audit reporting obligations the system's reporting module must meet?**

Yes — under our product liability framework we are required to produce an annual warranty claims summary by product category, which is submitted to our insurance underwriter. That report needs claim volumes, average resolution times, and claim approval rates. The new system needs an immutable claims record with a clear audit trail, and the reporting module needs to support point-in-time queries — "what did the data look like as of 31 December last year" — not just current snapshots.

**Q9: What would a well-designed warranty data model look like to you?**

At minimum: a Customer entity with a unique identifier and contact details. A Product entity with a unique model code, category, and warranty duration. A Warranty Registration entity linking Customer to Product, with registration date, purchase date, retailer, and a calculated expiry date. A Claim entity linked to a Warranty Registration, with submission date, fault category (structured picklist), fault description (free text supplement), status, resolution date, resolution outcome, and rejection reason if applicable. A Claim Event log capturing every status change with a timestamp and user ID. A Notification log recording every outbound communication. All foreign key relationships enforced at the database level, not just application level. And every critical field — particularly fault category, retailer, resolution outcome, and rejection reason — populated from a controlled vocabulary, not free text. That structure would answer 90% of the business questions we currently cannot answer.

### Interview: IT Security Officer (S11)

**Q1: What data protection regulations govern the handling of customer personal information in this system?**

We are subject to UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. The key obligations that apply directly to this system are: lawful basis for processing — we need to establish and document whether we're processing warranty data under a contractual necessity basis or a legitimate interests basis; data minimisation — we should only collect what is genuinely needed to operate the warranty process; storage limitation — personal data should not be kept longer than necessary; and data subject rights — customers have the right to access, rectify, and erase their data, and the system must be capable of fulfilling those requests. There is also a consent requirement specifically for any marketing use of registration data, which is separate from the warranty processing basis. Those two purposes must be clearly separated in the system's data handling — you cannot use warranty registration as a backdoor to build a marketing list without explicit consent.

**Q2: What is the organisation's current standard for access control in internal systems?**

Role-based access control is our standard. Least privilege — users get the minimum access necessary to perform their role. MFA is mandatory for any system that holds personal data, which this system clearly does. We use Azure AD as our identity provider for internal systems, and any new internal application must integrate with it rather than maintaining a separate credential store. Password policies are managed through Azure AD and are not negotiable at the application level. For privileged accounts — system administrators, anyone with database access — we require a separate privileged access workstation and session recording. Those requirements are non-negotiable for a system of this classification.

**Q3: What customer data will this system hold, and what are the retention and access requirements?**

The system will hold names, email addresses, postal addresses, phone numbers — PII class one. It will also hold purchase history and product ownership data — indirectly sensitive. And it will hold claim records including fault descriptions, which could in edge cases contain health-related information if a product fault caused an injury. I'd classify this system as moderate risk under our data classification framework. Retention: warranty records should be held for the warranty period plus 6 years to cover potential legal claims under the Limitation Act. After that, personal data should be anonymised or deleted, retaining only statistical records. Access: customer PII should be accessible only to roles that require it for operational purposes — claims processors, customer service, admin. Data Analysts should access anonymised or aggregated data only, not raw PII. The reporting module must be designed with that separation in mind.

**Q4: What threat vectors are particularly relevant to customer-facing warranty portals?**

Account enumeration — if the login or registration flow reveals whether an email address is registered or not, an attacker can harvest valid customer emails. The registration response must be identical whether or not an account exists. Insecure direct object references — if claim URLs contain a sequential claim ID like /claims/1234, a customer could increment the ID and view other customers' claims. All resource references must use non-sequential identifiers. Warranty fraud — submitting claims against serial numbers of products not owned by the claimant. We should consider rate limiting claim submissions per account and flagging anomalous patterns. File upload abuse — if customers can upload proof of purchase documents, the upload mechanism must validate file type and scan for malware. And credential stuffing — we should implement account lockout and ideally monitor for login attempts using known breached credential sets.

**Q5: What does your organisation's security review process look like during a development project?**

I conduct a security review at two points: at design sign-off, before development begins, and at pre-production, before go-live. At design stage I review the architecture diagram, the data flow diagram, and the access control model. I need those three artefacts to conduct the design review. At pre-production I conduct or commission a penetration test — for a customer-facing application of this classification, that's a mandatory step, not optional. The pen test report needs to be remediated and re-signed off before go-live. Lead time for an external pen test is typically 4 to 6 weeks from engagement to final report. That needs to be planned into the project schedule — it's not something you can compress at the end.

**Q6: If a data breach occurred, what is the regulatory notification obligation?**

Under UK GDPR, if a breach is likely to result in a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms, we are required to notify the ICO within 72 hours of becoming aware of it. If the breach is likely to result in a high risk to individuals — for example, large-scale exposure of PII or financial data — we must also notify affected individuals without undue delay. The system must support a forensic audit in that scenario: I need to be able to determine exactly which records were accessed, by whom, when, and what data was exposed. That requires comprehensive audit logging with tamper-resistant storage. The audit log cannot be something a compromised admin account can delete or modify. I'd recommend write-once log storage — either a separate logging service or a WORM-compliant storage configuration.

**Q7: What audit trail requirements apply?**

Every create, read, update, and delete operation on personal data must be logged with: timestamp, user ID, action type, and the record affected. For claims specifically, every status change must be logged with the user who made the change and any notes recorded. Read access to PII — viewing a customer's contact details — should be logged, not just write operations. Logs must be retained for a minimum of 3 years. Logs must be accessible to me and to designated compliance staff, but not modifiable by operational users or system administrators. And the logging system must generate an alert if logging itself fails — silent log failures are a serious audit risk. These requirements need to be in the functional specification so the development team builds them in from the start. Retrofitting audit logging after build is expensive and often incomplete.

**Q8: Are there third-party components that would require a data processing agreement?**

Yes — any email notification service we use will be processing customer personal data on our behalf, so a data processing agreement is required before go-live. If we use a cloud hosting provider — AWS, Azure — we already have DPAs with them, but they need to be reviewed to ensure the specific services used are covered and data residency commitments are met. If there's any third-party identity or fraud detection service used for customer authentication, that also requires a DPA. And if the development is being done by an external development partner, a DPA is required with them as well as appropriate security clauses in the contract. I'd want to review and approve the full list of third-party components before architecture is finalised. Discovering a third-party tool mid-build that hasn't been assessed is a significant compliance risk.

### Interview: System Administrator (S12)

**Q1: What does your current user access management process look like for internal systems?**

When a new staff member joins, their line manager submits a system access request form — it's a paper form, or sometimes just an email — to the IT helpdesk. I action it within 24 to 48 hours depending on queue. I create accounts manually in each system they need access to. For the warranty-related systems that currently exist, that's the shared drive folder, the email inbox access, and the CRM. Offboarding is the problem area. When someone leaves, HR notifies IT, but the notification often arrives after the person has already left — sometimes days after. We've had ex-employees with active system access for up to 5 days post-departure. For a system holding customer PII, that's a compliance risk I'm not comfortable with. Ideally the new system ties into our Azure AD provisioning so that disabling an AD account automatically revokes access everywhere.

**Q2: Will you be able to make configuration changes without developer involvement?**

That's a hard requirement for me. On our current systems, changing something as simple as a notification template requires raising a change request that goes to the development team, gets prioritised, gets built and tested, and deployed — a process that can take 3 to 4 weeks. We've had situations where a warranty period for a product changed and the system was quoting customers the wrong expiry date for a month because the update was in a development queue. Product-level warranty rules, notification templates, rejection reason codes, and user role assignments must all be configurable through an admin interface by a competent non-developer. If I have to raise a development ticket to change a product's warranty duration from 12 months to 24 months, this system will create as many problems as it solves.

**Q3: What does your monitoring and alerting setup look like for existing systems?**

We use Datadog for infrastructure monitoring across our main applications. I have dashboards for CPU, memory, disk I/O, and basic uptime for each service. Application-level monitoring is patchy — for some systems I get an alert when a service goes down, for others I find out when a user calls the helpdesk. For the warranty system I'd want: uptime monitoring with a page alert if the customer portal or the internal application is unresponsive for more than 2 minutes, error rate monitoring on the API layer, queue depth monitoring for the notification service — if emails are backing up undelivered I want to know before customers start calling — and a daily health check report that goes to my email so I have a baseline to compare against when something does go wrong.

**Q4: What diagnostic tools do you need to investigate user-reported problems?**

Access to application logs is the non-negotiable starting point. Currently for most of our systems I have server-level log access but not structured application logs, which means I'm grepping through raw text files hoping to find a relevant error. For this system I want structured, searchable logs — ideally centralised in our Datadog log management setup. I also need an admin interface that lets me look up a specific user account or claim record without going directly to the database, because direct database access creates its own risk and shouldn't be the first line of investigation. A basic admin panel where I can view a user's account status, their recent activity, and any error events on their session would resolve about 80% of first-line support queries without me needing to escalate to the development team.

**Q5: What does your current backup and recovery process look like?**

For our main database systems we take automated daily snapshots with a 30-day retention in AWS S3. Transaction logs allow point-in-time recovery within the last 7 days. For this system I'd expect the same minimum standard. RTO — I'd want to commit to a 4-hour recovery time objective, which is achievable with our current tooling if the infrastructure is designed correctly. RPO — maximum 1 hour of data loss, which means transaction log backup frequency needs to be at least every 60 minutes. The recovery process should be documented and tested at least annually — I've been on teams where the backup existed but nobody had ever actually tested the restore, and the one time they needed it, it turned out the snapshots had been silently failing for three months. I won't accept an untested backup process as a go-live condition.

**Q6: How are software updates and patches managed?**

We have a monthly patching window — second Tuesday of each month — for non-critical updates. Critical security patches are applied within 72 hours of release, after a rapid test on our staging environment. Changes are approved by the IT Change Advisory Board — it's a lightweight process for low-risk changes and a more formal review for anything high-risk. The same process should apply to this system. I'd want a staging environment that mirrors production so that patches can be validated before deployment. And I want a rollback procedure documented for every deployment — if a patch breaks something in production, I need to be able to roll back within 30 minutes without waiting for developer availability.

**Q7: What has caused the most operational headaches on systems you currently manage?**

Missing documentation. I've inherited three systems over the years where the original developers have left and there is no documentation of how they work, what the configuration parameters mean, or what the database schema represents. When something breaks, I'm reverse-engineering the system from live production data, which is stressful and error-prone. The second is configuration that's hardcoded into the application rather than externalised — database connection strings, email server settings, API keys baked into the code. When those need to change — and they always eventually need to change — it requires a developer. Third is scheduled jobs that run silently and fail silently — batch processes or notification jobs that fail and nobody knows until a user reports that they haven't received something. All scheduled jobs should log their outcome and alert on failure.

**Q8: What level of documentation do you need at handover?**

At minimum: a system architecture diagram, a database schema with table and field descriptions, a complete list of all external integrations and their dependencies, a user access matrix showing which roles exist and what permissions each has, a runbook for common administrative tasks — user provisioning, configuration changes, certificate renewals — and a troubleshooting guide covering the most likely failure scenarios and their resolution steps. I also want a documented deployment procedure and rollback procedure. I've been handed systems with a README that says "contact the developer if anything breaks." That is not acceptable for a production system holding customer PII. If the development team is no longer available six months after go-live and something breaks at 2am, I need to be able to fix it.

### Interview: Legal / Compliance Officer (S13)

**Q1: What consumer protection or warranty legislation applies, and what obligations does it create?**

In the UK, the Consumer Rights Act 2015 is the primary piece of legislation we need to satisfy — it sets out statutory rights that apply regardless of whatever warranty terms we publish. Products must be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose, and as described. Our contractual warranty operates on top of those statutory rights, not instead of them. The system must never present warranty registration as a prerequisite for statutory rights, because that would be misleading under the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008. There's also the Electronic Commerce Regulations 2002, which govern how contractual terms are presented online — customers must be able to access, save, and print the warranty terms at the point of registration. And the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013 have implications for how we communicate the warranty process to customers at point of sale. The system needs to be designed with all of these in mind, not just the operational workflow.

**Q2: Are warranty terms currently standardised, or do they vary?**

They vary, and that's a significant compliance risk we've been aware of for some time. We have different warranty periods for different product categories. We have different exclusion terms for products sold through certain retail channels versus our own direct sales. We have an extended warranty offered as a purchase add-on that has slightly different terms again. And we have some legacy products still in the field that were sold under older warranty terms that no longer reflect our current standard. The system needs to hold the correct warranty terms for each product at the time of sale, not just the current terms — because a customer who bought a product two years ago is entitled to the terms that were in place at the point of purchase, not the terms we updated to last year. That versioning requirement is often overlooked and it's legally important.

**Q3: What constitutes legally sufficient proof of purchase?**

A retail receipt showing the product, the purchase date, the retailer name, and the price. An online order confirmation from a verifiable source. A bank or credit card statement showing the transaction — acceptable as supplementary evidence, not primary. A gift receipt is more complicated — it evidences the transaction but not the purchaser, which affects how warranty rights are assigned. The system should be designed to accept digital uploads of proof of purchase documents and retain them immutably — they must not be modifiable after submission. If we ever need to defend a claim rejection in a consumer tribunal, we must be able to produce the original proof of purchase document exactly as submitted. A system that allows documents to be overwritten or deleted would undermine our ability to evidence our decisions.

**Q4: If a customer disputes a warranty rejection, what evidence would the system need to produce?**

The original claim submission with all data and documents as submitted, timestamped. The decision record — who made the decision, when, and what the documented reason was. Any communications sent to the customer. Any notes or rationale recorded during the review process. The warranty terms that were in effect at the time of purchase — which, as I noted, may differ from current terms. And the audit trail showing every action taken on the claim from submission to close. A consumer tribunal will expect a coherent, timestamped record of events. If our decision was reasonable and our process was followed, that record protects us. If the record is incomplete, inconsistent, or missing, we are exposed regardless of whether our decision was substantively correct. The system must make it trivially easy to produce a complete, ordered claim history on demand.

**Q5: What data retention obligations apply to warranty records?**

Warranty registration data should be retained for the warranty period plus a statutory minimum of 6 years — to cover contract claims under the Limitation Act 1980. Claim records, including all associated documents and communications, should be retained for the same period from the date of claim closure. After the retention period, personal data should be deleted or anonymised — we cannot indefinitely hold personal information beyond its lawful purpose. The system needs an automated retention management function — not a manual process where someone reviews records and decides what to delete, but a defined retention policy applied automatically with exceptions handled through a documented review process. I also want an annual data audit report from the system showing what data is held, its age, and its retention status.

**Q6: What marketing and communications constraints need to be embedded in the system?**

Any outbound communication that goes beyond what is strictly necessary for the warranty service — promotional content, product recommendations, survey invitations — requires explicit opt-in consent, separate from the warranty registration itself. The registration form cannot bundle warranty processing consent and marketing consent into a single checkbox. They must be separate, clearly labelled, and the warranty service must function fully regardless of whether the customer opts into marketing. Customers must be able to withdraw marketing consent at any time through a clear, accessible mechanism — an unsubscribe link in every marketing communication is the minimum standard. And we must maintain a consent record — when consent was given, what was consented to, through what mechanism — so that if a customer disputes that they consented to something, we can evidence our position. That consent record must be held in the system, not in a separate marketing database.

**Q7: Who has authority to make a final determination when warranty coverage is disputed?**

For standard disputes — customer disagrees with a rejection based on the documented terms — the Warranty Operations Manager has that authority within defined parameters. For cases where the terms are ambiguous, where the product was sold under unusual conditions, or where the customer is threatening legal action, the matter should come to me. I provide a legal interpretation of the applicable terms, and if necessary I work with the Operations Manager to reach a position. That decision and the full reasoning behind it must be recorded on the claim record — not just the outcome, but the reasoning. If we ever face a regulatory investigation into our claims handling practices, a record that shows "rejected — manager approved" without any documented rationale is significantly more damaging than a record with a clear, reasoned explanation, even if the outcome was the same.

**Q8: Have there been previous legal disputes or compliance recommendations that this system is designed to prevent?**

Yes, and I'll be direct about it. Two years ago we had a customer take us to the Small Claims Court over a rejected warranty claim for a product that was within the statutory rights period under the Consumer Rights Act. We lost — not because our decision was wrong, but because we couldn't produce a coherent record of our claims process. The judge noted that our documentation was inadequate. We received a compliance recommendation from the ICO last year following a subject access request that we couldn't fulfil fully because warranty records were held in physical files and the relevant documents couldn't be located. Both of those incidents are on record and the expectation from our DPO is that the new system demonstrably addresses both. This is not a discretionary requirement — it is a direct response to documented compliance failures.

### Interview: Marketing Manager (S14)

**Q1: From a brand perspective, what should the warranty registration experience feel like?**

It should feel like a natural extension of the purchase experience — a moment where the brand is reinforcing the customer's confidence in their decision. Right now, if a customer even attempts to register, they're confronted with a paper card that looks like it was designed in the early 2000s. That's not consistent with how we present the brand anywhere else. The digital registration experience should be clean, quick, and reassuring — it should feel like the brand is saying "we've got you covered" rather than "please fill in this form so we can file it." It's a touchpoint, and like every touchpoint it either builds or erodes customer trust. The registration confirmation message in particular — the email or screen the customer sees after completing registration — is an opportunity to reinforce the brand voice, not just send a system receipt. I want input into that content.

**Q2: What customer data beyond the minimum would be commercially valuable to capture at registration?**

Product usage context would be valuable — domestic versus commercial, household size, primary use case. That data would help us understand product-market fit at a segment level. Preferred contact channel is useful — email, SMS, phone — so our communications are more likely to reach people in the way they prefer. And an optional question about where they heard about the product or which retailer they purchased from would give us channel attribution data that we currently struggle to collect consistently. I'm aware that all of this is subject to consent requirements, and I've spoken with Legal about it. Any data collected beyond the minimum warranty requirement must be opt-in and clearly explained. I'm not interested in capturing data we can't use legitimately — it creates compliance risk without business value.

**Q3: Are warranty expiry notifications used as a prompt for repurchase or upsell?**

Currently, no — and that's a missed opportunity. If a customer's warranty is expiring, that means they've had the product for 1 to 3 years, they're a known, registered customer, and they're potentially in a purchase consideration window. An expiry notification that simply says "your warranty has expired" is a wasted communication. We could be including a product upgrade offer, an extended warranty option, or a loyalty discount. I'd want the system to support parameterised notification templates that can include product-specific or segment-specific content, so that the expiry notification for a premium product can include a relevant upgrade message while the notification for a budget product can include a satisfaction check or replacement prompt. That requires coordination between the system and our promotional calendar — something to design in from the start rather than retrofit.

**Q4: Is there a requirement for the customer portal to be on-brand?**

Absolutely, and this is non-negotiable from my side. The portal needs to use our brand colour palette, typography, logo, and tone of voice guidelines. It cannot look like a generic off-the-shelf system with our logo slapped on top — I've seen that done on other projects and it's immediately jarring to customers who know the brand. Our brand guidelines are documented and I'll provide them to the project team. The domain should ideally be a subdomain of our main website — something like warranty.ourbrand.com — rather than a third-party domain, because a different domain undermines customer confidence and increases the chance they'll think it's a phishing attempt. Final approval of all customer-facing visual design goes through me before go-live. That's not about controlling the process — it's about protecting the brand.

**Q5: Are there customer segments you'd want to track separately for marketing analysis?**

Yes. Customers who register within 7 days of purchase are a high-engagement segment — they're organised, brand-involved, and likely to be receptive to loyalty communications. Customers who register because they're making a claim are a different segment — they may be frustrated and that requires a different communications approach. Customers who register a premium product versus an entry-level product represent different value segments. And customers who have registered multiple products are a loyalty segment we should be nurturing differently from single-product owners. For that segmentation to be meaningful in our marketing platform, the registration system needs to capture timestamps, product tier, and registration trigger — did they register proactively at purchase, or only when prompted by an expiry reminder? Those context fields need to be part of the data model.

**Q6: What would a high versus low warranty registration rate tell you commercially?**

A high registration rate tells me that customers trust the brand enough to take a post-purchase action — that's a signal of purchase satisfaction and brand affinity. It also gives us a richer first-party data set, which is increasingly valuable as third-party cookies disappear. A low registration rate could mean several things: the process is too difficult (which is a UX problem), customers don't see the value in registering (which is a messaging problem), or we're attracting lower-engagement customers through certain channels (which is a channel mix problem). I'd want to monitor registration rates by purchase channel, product category, and time from purchase — not just overall registration volume. Those breakdowns would tell us where the friction or motivation gaps are, and they'd inform where we focus improvement efforts.

**Q7: Are there regulatory constraints on using warranty registration data for marketing?**

Yes, and Legal has been very clear with me about this. Under UK GDPR and the Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations, we need specific consent to send marketing communications by email or SMS. That consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous — it cannot be bundled into the warranty registration acceptance or buried in terms and conditions. In practice that means the registration form needs a clearly separate, unticked checkbox for marketing opt-in, with plain language explaining what the customer is agreeing to. The system must record that consent with a timestamp so we can evidence it. And customers must be able to withdraw consent at any time, with that withdrawal reflected in the system immediately. I've seen projects where the marketing and legal requirements around consent were designed inconsistently and it created a mess to unpick. I'd rather get it right in the requirements phase.
